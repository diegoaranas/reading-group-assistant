## TL;DR

Thoreau argues that individual conscience, not the will of the majority, is the
true measure of right action, and that a just person must refuse cooperation
with a government that perpetrates injustice. Written after his night in jail for
refusing a tax that funded slavery and the Mexican–American War, the essay makes
the case for principled, nonviolent resistance to the state.

## Central claims / arguments

- "That government is best which governs least" — government is at best a
  convenient expedient, easily abused.
- Conscience outranks law: the citizen's first obligation is to do what is right,
  not what is legal.
- The machinery of the state should be resisted — by withdrawing cooperation —
  when it makes you "the agent of injustice to another."
- A minority of one, acting on principle, has moral force the majority lacks.

## Outline

1. **The case against the state** — government as expedient; the danger of
   deferring to the majority merely because it is strong.
2. **Conscience vs. law** — soldiers and citizens as machines; the duty to not
   lend yourself to wrong.
3. **The act of resistance** — refusing taxes; "Under a government which
   imprisons any unjustly, the true place for a just man is also a prison."
4. **Night in jail** — the personal anecdote that grounds the argument.
5. **The relationship rebuilt** — a vision of a state that respects the
   individual.

## Key terms

| Term | Meaning (as used here) |
|---|---|
| Expedient | A temporary, practical arrangement — not a moral end in itself. |
| Majority rule | Decision by numbers/force, which Thoreau distinguishes from justice. |
| Conscience | The individual's moral judgment, which he holds supreme over law. |
| Civil disobedience | Deliberate, nonviolent refusal to obey unjust laws. |

## Notable quotes

> "That government is best which governs least." — opening paragraph

> "Under a government which imprisons any unjustly, the true place for a just man
> is also a prison." — section on resistance

> "Any man more right than his neighbors constitutes a majority of one already." — on conscience

## Read with these questions in mind

1. Where does Thoreau draw the line between obeying the law and obeying
   conscience — and does he ever say the line is hard to find?
2. Is withdrawing cooperation (not paying taxes) a sufficient form of resistance,
   or does it let the individual off too easily?
3. How does the jail anecdote change the force of the abstract argument?
