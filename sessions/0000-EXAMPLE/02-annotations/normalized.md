## Ada (`ada-email.txt`)

- **On the "majority of one" line (~p.3):** Doing a lot of work; unconvinced he
  earns it — reads as rhetorical flourish more than argument.
- **On the marching-soldiers image:** Loves it; "the whole essay in one image"
  (soldiers marching "against their wills").
- **Question raised:** Is tax refusal actually resistance, or just a way to keep
  your own hands clean while the injustice continues?

## Bilal (`chat-dump.md`)

- **On the opening line:** "Government is best which governs least" reads as
  libertarian boilerplate today, but was likely sharper in 1849.
- **Question raised:** He never says what happens if *everyone* disobeys —
  doesn't that dissolve into chaos?
- **Synthesis remark:** The abstraction is both the weakness and the source of the
  essay's staying power.

## Chen (`chat-dump.md`)

- **Reframing:** Thoreau isn't anti-government, he's anti-*deference*; the night
  in jail is the key, and he's "weirdly cheerful" about it.
- **Observation:** He barely mentions slavery directly despite it being the whole
  context.

## Dara (`dara-markup.pdf`, annotated text)

- **On the opening line ("that government is best which governs least"):** He opens
  with the conclusion, not an argument — assumes what he needs to prove.
- **On "the true place for a just man is also a prison":** The moral core. But
  "place" is metaphorical — is this duty, or just consolation?
- **On "a majority of one":** Compare Mill, *On Liberty* — the individual against
  the majority. Thoreau gets there first.
